How Cairn protects your files

Two jobs, one fingerprint

Cairn does two things, and they lean on the same idea: a tiny fingerprint of each file.

  • It watches your files and warns you when something changes. If a file goes missing, gets altered, or quietly corrupts, Cairn notices and tells you, before the damage works its way through your backups. This is what Cairn was built for.
  • It can prove a file existed on a certain date. For files where that matters, Cairn records a tamper-proof timestamp on the Bitcoin blockchain that anyone can check, without trusting you or Cairn.

The first job is the important one. The second is a powerful bonus that's essential for some files and simply nice to have for the rest. Together they cover both halves of the question “are my files still exactly what they should be, and can I prove it?”

Job one: catching silent loss before your backups do

Some files you open every day. Others — a folder of your kids' photos, old tax records, scanned documents — you almost never touch, but you would be devastated to lose. Those are exactly the files that can rot or vanish without anyone noticing.

The quiet danger is that backups copy faithfully — including the damage. If a file is silently deleted, overwritten, or corrupted and you don't catch it, your backups keep dutifully copying the broken version. Eventually the last good copy rotates out, and the loss is permanent. By the time you finally open the file, there's nothing left to restore from.

Cairn closes that gap. It keeps a fingerprint of every file it watches and re-checks them on a schedule. The moment a file goes missing, is modified, or quietly corrupts on disk — even when its size and date look untouched — the fingerprint stops matching, and Cairn flags it and can alert you. You find out while a good copy still exists, when you can still do something about it.

{{ m.icon("info", size=15, cls="text-3") }} It only watches; it never touches. Cairn reads your files to fingerprint them and cannot change or delete what it monitors. Recovering a flagged file is up to you and your backups — Cairn's job is to make sure you know in time.

Job two: proving a file existed, to anyone

How do you prove that a document existed on a certain date, exactly as it is now and not edited later? And how do you prove it to someone who has no reason to take your word for it: an auditor, a court, a client, or your future self? The old answers (a notary, mailing yourself a sealed envelope, a dated email) all ask someone to trust a third party. Cairn gives you proof that rests on mathematics and a public record no one can quietly change. The next few steps show how, starting with that same fingerprint.

1

A fingerprint of the file (the “hash”)

When Cairn first sees a file, it runs the contents through a standard calculation called SHA-256. Out comes a short string of letters and numbers: a hash, or digital fingerprint, that looks like this:

e3b0c44298fc1c149afbf4c8996fb92427ae41e4649b934ca495991b7852b855
  • It's unique to the contents. Change a single comma, pixel, or byte and the fingerprint changes completely. That's what lets Cairn spot a file that's been altered or has quietly corrupted (job one), and what makes a timestamp prove a file hasn't changed since (job two).
  • It's one-way. You can't turn the fingerprint back into the file, so publishing it reveals nothing about what's inside.
  • It's reproducible. Anyone, anywhere, using any tool, gets the exact same fingerprint from the same file. That's what makes it checkable by others.
2

A timestamp recorded on Bitcoin

Cairn then publishes that fingerprint into the Bitcoin blockchain using OpenTimestamps, a free and open standard built for exactly this purpose. The blockchain is a public ledger maintained by thousands of independent computers worldwide. Once something is written into it, it is permanent, visible to everyone, and cannot be backdated or quietly edited. Rewriting it would mean overpowering the entire global network.

Because each Bitcoin block carries a trustworthy time, recording your fingerprint in a block proves the file must have existed before that block was created. Only the fingerprint is ever published, never the file.

{{ m.icon("clock", size=15, cls="text-3") }} “Pending” vs. “Anchored.” A new fingerprint is bundled with many others and waits for the next confirmed Bitcoin block. Until that lands it reads pending; once it's in a block it reads anchored and carries a confirmed date. This usually takes a few hours, and it's free: there's no per-file fee.
3

Anyone can check it, without trusting Cairn

The proof Cairn keeps for each file (a small .ots file) is self-contained. Anyone holding the file and its .ots proof can confirm the file existed in its exact form by a given date, using free, open-source tools, with no account and no help from Cairn. The guarantee rests on Bitcoin and mathematics, not on Cairn or any single company staying online. If Cairn vanished tomorrow, your existing proofs would still verify.

What it proves, and what it doesn't

{{ m.icon("check", size=15) }} It proves
  • This exact file existed on or before a specific date.
  • It has not been altered since it was recorded.
{{ m.icon("x", size=15) }} It does not prove
  • Who created or owns the file.
  • That the file's contents are true or accurate.

Verify a proof yourself

You don't have to use Cairn to check a proof. On any verified file's page, click Download .ots proof, then confirm it independently:

  • Drag the file and its .ots proof onto the public verifier at opentimestamps.org, or
  • Run the open-source command-line tool: ots verify yourfile.pdf.ots
{{ m.icon("info", size=15, cls="text-3") }} The proof file contains only your file's fingerprint and the path back to its Bitcoin block, not the file itself. It's safe to share with anyone you want to convince.

Common questions

Do I have to timestamp my files to use the watch?
No. The two jobs are independent. Cairn can watch a set of files for loss and corruption without ever recording a timestamp, and you can turn on timestamping only for the files where proof-of-date matters.
What if a file changed because I meant to change it?
Cairn just flags that the file is different — it can't know whether you intended it. When a change is expected, you accept it as the new baseline, and Cairn watches the updated version from then on.
Does Cairn upload my files anywhere?
No. Your files stay on your server. Only the fingerprint, which reveals nothing about the contents, is ever published.
What happens to my proofs if Cairn shuts down?
They keep working. Each .ots proof can be verified independently with open-source tools, forever, with no dependency on Cairn.
How long until a new proof is confirmed?
Usually a few hours, once your fingerprint is included in a Bitcoin block. Until then it shows as pending.
Is this the same as a legal notary?
No. It's a strong, independently verifiable technical record of when a file existed. Whether and how that's used as evidence depends on your jurisdiction and situation.
